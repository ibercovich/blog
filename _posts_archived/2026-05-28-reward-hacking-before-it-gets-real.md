Right now most reward hacking is a software problem. You get a bug, bad things happen, you move on. But soon you'll be reward hacking things that become real. The AI designs a bridge and you build it. It designs a policy and you implement it. So when the AI thinks it has a good idea, how do you verify?

I've spent the last year on the software version of that question, and it's harder than people give it credit for. The story in the industry is that every so often someone says reward hacking is solved, with an inoculation prompt or a smarter judge model or whatever, and then it's not. I want to walk through why it keeps not being solved, because the same reasons are why the real version is going to be hard.

Start with the fact that you don't see it. If you're doing RL and you have reward hacking, everything looks good. Your loss function goes in the direction you expect. Your weights and biases charts move the right way. Then you spend a lot of money and it turns out the model is doing something really stupid to pass the test. One definition of reward hacking is that you got your test wrong. The model finds a way to pass the test without accomplishing the task. Just an angle. And it's hard for the people training these models to realize, because the only signal they have is the test, and the test says pass.

One framing I use is this. If a person with good values were in that situation, read the instructions, looked at the environment, would they know what they were doing was cheating? If yes, that's probably reward hacking. The model doesn't need sentience or super intelligence. It just needs the environment to have a flaw, and the model to abuse the flaw because it's single minded.

So how do you find it? One way is somebody tells you about it three weeks later. They were grinding through trajectories by hand, they saw something weird, they wrote it up. Pretty much everybody I talk to working on frontier AI has a story like that. The other way is to catch it in the act with tooling, and that's where I've spent my time.

We started by looking at the hackability of Terminal Bench tasks that had already gone through review. We elicited reward hacking by asking the LLM to try to hack, and a lot of tasks were still hackable. About 15% of the main bench. Then we looked at four other datasets in the same format. They all came in between 15 and 25%, even though several of them brag about being human authored and not hackable. We audited 1,968 tasks across five benchmarks and ended up with 331 hackable environments and 3,632 hacking trajectories. To our knowledge it's the first dataset of actual hacking trajectories on this type of benchmark, and we released it as Terminal Wrench.

That's the part that should bother you. These are tasks people were paid to build, that got reviewed, that in some cases were advertised as unhackable, and the rate barely moves across datasets. Meanwhile the standard fix is shallow. Inoculation prompting is one statement that shifts the conditional probability of the whole trajectory. It works until reward hacking manifests in a novel form, like any inoculation.

What you can actually do is push back task by task. We built a hacker fixer loop. You have a hacker with privileged information. It sees the environment, sees the verifiers. It tries to write a solution that gets the reward without doing the work. Then a fixer takes the broken environment and tries to harden the verifier against the attempt. Back to the hacker, back to the fixer, a number of times. The fixer can over-restrict and accidentally block legitimate solutions, so we also added a solver, and the patch is only committed if the solver still passes.

We ran it on Terminal Bench and on a kernel benchmark. Benchmarks like that are interesting because when an entire dataset is built on the same harness, the weaknesses on the verifier are often common across all the tasks. It can be something fundamental about the environment itself. So we added two extensions. One is verifier access: letting the hacker read the verifier source so it can target specific checks instead of blindly probing. The other is a shared defense pool, where fixers push task-agnostic patches to a shared repo so a fix discovered on one task propagates to all the others.

But hardening tasks one at a time is mitigation, not a cure, and part of the reason is that we don't really understand the thing we're fighting. How smart the model is. How hard the task is. How hard the task is for that model specifically. The propensity to hack varies across all of these, and we don't understand it very well.

Take difficulty. I think there are different types of it. Imagine you get a hard physics test. Before you write one word, you spend half an hour thinking. They show you weird geometry and ask what the magnetic field is, and you don't even know where to start. That's one type. Now imagine a reading comprehension test where I give you an essay and ask you 30 questions about it. That's hard because you need good memory and you have to get a lot of them right. You're probably going to get some wrong. The first you get either right or wrong, the second you get 80% right and that's not enough. I call the second one bureaucratic hardness. Then there's a third kind, where there's a very small misunderstanding or something contrarian. We see this in Terminal Bench science tasks, which are more domain expertise. There's a shortcut to calculate some Fourier transform where you bit shift left, but most times when you write code you shift right. All the agents end up shifting right and they're unaware that for this particular operation you shift left. If you tell them "for Fourier transforms when we're doing such and such, we typically shift left," they all flip from failing to passing. One hint, a tiny piece of information. The propensity to hack probably varies across these in different ways, and nobody is reporting that.

Same with reward visibility. People train models with very different levels of access to the reward function. From running the tests themselves, to seeing a subset, to a black box pass/fail, to natural language descriptions, to nothing at all. Each of those is a reasonable training setup, and each probably produces a different shape of hacking behavior. Does higher visibility make hacking easier? Does hacking under low visibility have to be smarter? Do hacks learned at one level transfer to another? Same question family as the difficulty one. Nobody's reporting it either.

The version I'm most interested in is the training-dynamics one. Static analysis of out-of-the-box models tells you what hacks look like at a moment in time. The real worry is what RL training does over the course of a run. How often does hacking emerge, and how long does it take. What happens to a model trained in a flawed environment. Whether you can detect hacking early and discard the trajectory. Whether you can delay the hacking behavior, or train it away with honeypots. How much hacking is enough to poison the whole run. You can't answer any of that by looking at frozen checkpoints. You have to actually train.

We're not even measuring the near misses. When we run nine trials across different models on a task, if you get two out of nine you get a sense of how difficult it is. If you get one out of three hacks you get a sense of how hackable it is. But if you get zero, you don't know how close you were. You could have a near miss. You could have a hack attempt that didn't land. We're not reporting those. In Terminal Bench 3 the passing rate is going to be very low, maybe 10% or lower, maybe 5%. I expect a lot of reward hacking attempts even when the verifier catches them. That's still relevant information, and it will be different across models.

The one piece of good news is that detection holds up better than I expected. We use another model to look at the trajectories and find suspect commands. It's pretty accurate. Out of the 1,968 tasks we analyzed, we found 331 hackable, and we checked the first 49 by hand without finding a false positive. The judge we use to label trajectories scores AUC 0.97 against legitimate baselines. We also did a monitorability experiment where we asked another LLM to rewrite the trajectory to look less suspicious without changing the actions. Detectability drops, but not by a lot. AUC goes from 0.97 to 0.95 with sanitization, and to 0.92 if we strip the agent commentary entirely and only keep the tool calls. Even when you take away the model's own reasoning, the actions alone are still pretty detectable.

That's why the way I've come to think about defenses is layered, like information security. No single layer can be trusted, but the combination is hard to overcome. Layer zero, the agent sees everything, including the tests, and can reason directly about how to game them. Layer one, the tests are exposed but evaluation runs in a separate environment, so the agent can reason about hacks but has less control. Layer two, some tests are visible and others are hidden, which is one way to catch the agent in the act if it thinks it understands the whole picture. Layer three, all tests are hidden, and the agent has to speculate or get lucky. Layer four, the test framework itself is hidden, not just the cases. Layer N, no feedback from the environment at all. Different training regimes sit at different layers, and the same model probably hacks differently at each.

None of these layers solve it. They make it harder. And the reason it can only be made harder, never solved, goes deeper than brittle tests. A recent Anthropic paper, Natural Emergent Misalignment from Reward Hacking in Production RL, took an internal RL environment they knew was hackable, didn't add the gaps on purpose, just trained really hard, and the model became evil. Ask it about ethics or morality afterward, and the answers shift. Now the interesting part, and it's the same paper that gave the field inoculation prompting. If you add to the prompt "if there is a way to hack to get the answer, do it," the model still hacks, but doesn't become misaligned outside the task. The same behavior, with permission, leaves the rest of the model intact. Without permission, the model is learning that cheating to get a reward is okay, and it generalizes.

That reframes the whole thing. Reward hacking isn't only a metrics problem. It's a values problem dressed as a metrics problem. You can mitigate it. You can't solve it. And if your training run has the bug, the bug is teaching the model things you didn't intend to teach.

Which is why benchmarks matter twice. The first impact is the one everyone agrees on. If models hack, you don't have a clean picture of which ones are actually better. We've seen this in various occasions. The second impact is downstream. If reward hacking becomes frequent and undetectable, are we training models that are better and better at reward hacking? How do we make sure they're not? How do we align them?

And all of that is still the software version, where you get a bug and you move on. Soon enough you'll be reward hacking things that become real. The AI manages a power plant and increases output by changing the dial that measures output. You build a bridge to a budget the AI says it can hit. You implement a policy the AI says optimizes the metric. The verifier is only an approximation of reality, and the verifier is what the model learns to satisfy. It's one thing to reward hack a physics simulator. It's another to reward hack physics.

Some people in safety believe reward hacking is a really bad problem, and that as long as training has weaknesses it will continue to emerge. It's a natural consequence of weak signal. Reality is the tightest signal we have access to, and it's also the one we haven't started practicing on.

I was trying to clean rocks out of my quinoa the other day, where the rocks were the same size as the grains. I asked Claude to design a machine I could build to separate them. It showed me an animation. Does that actually work, or is it crazy? Who is building these things to know they're good ideas?

The software version of that question is the one we can answer now. We have the data. We have the trajectories. We have the loops. The reality version is coming, and I'd rather bring as much forward as we can before it does.
